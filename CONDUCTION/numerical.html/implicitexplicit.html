🧮 Explicit and Implicit Schemes in Heat Conduction

🔍 Introduction

In transient heat conduction problems, numerical methods are employed when analytical solutions are difficult. The finite difference method (FDM) discretizes time and space to solve the heat equation. Two common time integration schemes are:

  • 🔹 Explicit Scheme (Forward in Time)
  • 🔹 Implicit Scheme (Backward in Time)

📘 Heat Equation and Discretization

The one-dimensional transient conduction equation:

\[ \frac{\partial T}{\partial t} = \alpha \frac{\partial^2 T}{\partial x^2} \]

Using finite difference, it is discretized as:

\[ \frac{T_i^{n+1} - T_i^n}{\Delta t} = \alpha \frac{T_{i+1}^n - 2T_i^n + T_{i-1}^n}{\Delta x^2} \]

⏩ Explicit Method

In the explicit scheme, the future temperature is calculated directly using current values:

\[ T_i^{n+1} = T_i^n + \frac{\alpha \Delta t}{\Delta x^2} (T_{i+1}^n - 2T_i^n + T_{i-1}^n) \]

The explicit scheme is conditionally stable and requires the Fourier number:

\[ Fo = \frac{\alpha \Delta t}{\Delta x^2} \leq \frac{1}{2} \]

⏪ Implicit Method

In the implicit scheme, future temperatures appear on both sides and a system of equations must be solved:

\[ T_i^{n+1} - Fo (T_{i+1}^{n+1} - 2T_i^{n+1} + T_{i-1}^{n+1}) = T_i^n \]

This method is unconditionally stable and suitable for larger time steps, but involves solving a linear system at each time step.

📊 Comparison

  • ✅ Explicit: Simple, fast, conditionally stable
  • ✅ Implicit: Stable for any time step, but computationally expensive
  • ✅ Implicit preferred for stiff systems and long-time simulations

🏭 Applications

  • Simulating heat flow in walls, fins, and complex geometries
  • Transient thermal behavior in electronics
  • Modeling startup and shutdown of thermal systems
  • Engineering software like ANSYS, MATLAB, and OpenFOAM
