📉 One-Term Approximation Method

❓ What is the One-Term Approximation?

When Biot number (Bi > 0.1) and Fourier number (Fo > 0.2) apply, the Heisler chart can be replaced with a simple exponential equation:

θ/θi = A × exp(–λ² × Fo)

Here, A and λ are constants based on the geometry:

  • Plane Wall: A = 1, λ = π
  • Cylinder: A = 1.27, λ = 1.0
  • Sphere: A = 1.36, λ = 1.0

⚙️ Example Calculation

Given:

  • Material: Metal rod (cylinder)
  • Radius = 1 cm
  • Initial T = 200°C
  • Ambient T = 25°C
  • Fo = 0.3
  • Bi = 0.2

Steps:

  1. A = 1.27, λ = 1.0
  2. θ/θi = 1.27 × exp(–1² × 0.3) ≈ 0.94
  3. T = 25 + (200–25) × 0.94 ≈ 190°C

🔍 Why Does One-Term Approximation Work?

The full transient conduction solution involves an infinite series of exponential terms. However, for Fo > 0.2, the higher-order terms decay rapidly and contribute very little. So, we keep just the first term for a simpler but reasonably accurate solution.

Full solution:
θ/θi = Σ An × exp(–λn² Fo)

One-term approximation:
θ/θi ≈ A × exp(–λ² Fo)

⚖️ How Accurate Is It?

  • Error < 5% for Fo > 0.2
  • Highly accurate for long-time predictions
  • Less accurate at the start of cooling (Fo < 0.2)

🔬 Physical Insight Behind the One-Term Approximation

When a body starts cooling, it loses heat from its surface. As time progresses, temperature differences inside the body begin to level out. In this situation, only the slowest-decaying mode (the first term in the series solution) dominates the temperature profile. This is the essence of the one-term approximation.

  • Applies to semi-infinite bodies or thick objects
  • Valid when internal conduction dominates surface effects
  • Saves time without large loss of accuracy

📏 Geometries Where One-Term Works

One-term approximation is derived for specific geometries with symmetry:

  • 🔹 Plane Wall (slab) – thickness 2L
  • 🔹 Long Cylinder – radius r₀
  • 🔹 Sphere – radius r₀

In each case, the solution involves a different λ value and coefficient A.

📘 Mathematical Form (One-Term Approximation)

Temperature ratio is calculated as:

θ/θi = A × exp(–λ² × Fo)

Here’s how λ and A vary by geometry:

Geometry λ A
Plane Wall π/2 1
Cylinder 1st root of J0 From chart or table
Sphere π 6/π²

📉 When One-Term Fails — Use Heisler Charts

If your Biot number is:

  • Bi < 0.1 → Use lumped capacitance model
  • Bi > 0.1 and Fo < 0.2 → One-term may be inaccurate

In those cases, the full Heisler charts give better predictions.

✅ Advantages & ❌ Limitations

✅ Pros:

  • Fast estimation without charts
  • Easy to use in hand calculations
  • Great for steady Bi and simple geometry

❌ Cons:

  • Less accurate for short times (Fo < 0.2)
  • Only for Bi > 0.1
  • Only applies to constant surface temperature problems
