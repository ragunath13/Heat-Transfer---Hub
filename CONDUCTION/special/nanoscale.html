üìò Introduction

Nanoscale conduction refers to heat transfer that occurs at scales where classical Fourier's law breaks down due to quantum and size-dependent effects. This typically happens when characteristic lengths are on the order of nanometers (1‚Äì100 nm), such as in nanowires, thin films, and carbon nanotubes.

At such scales, heat carriers (phonons, electrons) experience boundary scattering and ballistic transport, requiring new models for heat conduction.

üß™ Limitations of Fourier's Law

Classical Fourier‚Äôs Law assumes diffusive transport, which breaks down when:

  • Mean free path of heat carriers \( \lambda \) is comparable to system length \( L \).
  • Scattering events are infrequent and boundary scattering dominates.
  • Time scales of interest are shorter than phonon relaxation time.

In such cases, Fourier‚Äôs law:

\[ q = -k \nabla T \]

fails to accurately describe the heat flux \( q \).

üìê Ballistic vs Diffusive Conduction

In diffusive transport, heat carriers undergo frequent collisions and follow Fourier‚Äôs law. In ballistic transport (common in nanoscale systems), carriers travel without scattering, leading to non-local heat transfer:

\[ q = \frac{1}{L} \int_{0}^{L} k_{\text{eff}}(x) \left(-\frac{dT}{dx}\right) dx \]

Here, \( k_{\text{eff}} \) is position-dependent and reflects local material behavior and transport regime.

üß† Models for Nanoscale Conduction

  • Phonon Boltzmann Transport Equation (BTE)
  • Non-Equilibrium Molecular Dynamics (NEMD)
  • Effective Medium Approximations (EMA)
  • Landauer Approach for ballistic channels

These models incorporate quantum effects, phonon dispersion, and mean free path distributions to provide accurate thermal predictions.

üßä Thermal Conductivity Reduction

In nanostructures, thermal conductivity is reduced due to:

  • Boundary and interface scattering
  • Confinement of phonon modes
  • Phonon localization and interference
