Grashof-Prandtl Interactions

How the interplay between buoyancy and viscosity governs natural convection behavior

Grashof-Dominated Flow (Gr ≫ 1)

Characteristics:

  • Strong buoyancy forces overcome viscosity
  • Vigorous convection currents develop
  • Flow tends to become turbulent
  • Boundary layers become thinner
High Interaction

Prandtl-Dominated Flow (Pr ≫ 1)

Characteristics:

  • Momentum diffuses faster than heat
  • Thermal boundary layer thicker than velocity layer
  • Flow remains laminar at higher Gr
  • Heat transfer primarily by conduction
Low Interaction

Key Concept: Rayleigh Number (Ra = Gr·Pr)

The product of Grashof and Prandtl numbers determines the actual convection behavior. While Grashof represents buoyancy-to-viscosity ratio, Prandtl represents momentum-to-thermal diffusivity ratio. Their product Ra indicates the relative strength of buoyancy-driven convection.

Boundary Layer Characteristics

Velocity Boundary Layer

Thickness depends on Grashof number:

δv ≈ L/Gr1/4

Grows as 1⁄4 power of Gr

Thermal Boundary Layer

Thickness depends on Prandtl number:

δt ≈ δv/Pr1/3

For Pr > 1: δt < δv

Key Ratio: δt/δv ≈ Pr-1/3

This fundamental relationship explains why high-Pr fluids (oils) have much thinner thermal boundary layers than velocity boundary layers, while low-Pr fluids (liquid metals) show the opposite behavior.

The critical Rayleigh number for transition depends strongly on Pr:

  • Moderate Pr (≈1): Racrit ≈ 109

Design Implications

Low-Pr Fluids (Pr < 0.1)

  • Use high Grashof numbers (large ΔT or size)
  • Thin thermal boundary layers require precise modeling
  • Common in metallurgical processes

Intermediate-Pr (0.1 < Pr < 10)

  • Standard correlations apply
  • Most gases and water fall here
  • Balance between buoyancy and viscosity

High-Pr Fluids (Pr > 10)

  • Require special high-Pr correlations
  • Thermal boundary layer controls heat transfer
  • Common in oil cooling and chemical processes

Optimization Strategy

For maximum heat transfer:
1. Increase Gr via larger ΔT or characteristic length
2. Match fluid Pr to application requirements
3. Position surfaces vertically when possible
4. Maintain Ra in optimal range for flow regime

Case Study: Transformer Oil Cooling

Parameters for typical power transformer:

  • Oil Pr ≈ 100 (at operating temperature)
  • Characteristic height: 2m
  • ΔT ≈ 30K → Gr ≈ 5×1010
  • Ra = Gr·Pr ≈ 5×1012

Resulting behavior:

  1. Extremely thick thermal boundary layer (≈5mm)
  2. Fully turbulent flow despite high viscosity
  3. Nu ≈ 200 → h ≈ 50 W/m²K
