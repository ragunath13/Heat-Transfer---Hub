Nusselt Number in Heat Transfer

What is the Nusselt Number?

The Nusselt Number (Nu) is a dimensionless number representing the ratio of convective to conductive heat transfer across a fluid boundary:

Nu = h·L / k

h: Convective heat transfer coefficient (W/m²·K) L: Characteristic length (m) k: Thermal conductivity (W/m·K)

Physical Meaning:

  • If Nu = 1, heat transfer is purely by conduction
  • If Nu > 1, convection enhances heat transfer
  • Higher Nusselt numbers indicate more efficient convective transfer
Correlations and Flow Types

Laminar Flow

  • Smooth flow layers, lower Nu
  • Typical correlation: Nu = 3.66 (constant wall temp, fully developed)
  • Dominated by conduction at the boundary

Transition

  • Unstable flow behavior
  • Nu increases as convection becomes dominant
  • Correlation depends on Re and Pr

Turbulent Flow

  • High velocity, chaotic mixing
  • Greatly enhances convection: Nu ≫ 1
  • Example correlation: Nu = 0.023·Re0.8·Pr0.3

What is the Nusselt Number?

The Nusselt Number (Nu) is a dimensionless parameter that quantifies the enhancement of heat transfer through a fluid layer as a result of convection compared to conduction alone. It is one of the key nondimensional groups in convective heat transfer analysis.

Nu = h·L / k

h: Convective heat transfer coefficient (W/m²·K) L: Characteristic length (m) k: Thermal conductivity (W/m·K)

Physical Meaning:

  • If Nu = 1, heat transfer is by pure conduction — as if the fluid were stationary.
  • If Nu > 1, convection enhances the transfer by mixing and fluid motion.
  • A larger Nusselt number indicates more effective convection and thinner thermal boundary layers.

Why is Nusselt Number Important?

  • It helps engineers evaluate and design efficient heat exchangers, pipe flows, and cooling systems.
  • Nu bridges theoretical analysis and experimental data by correlating it with other dimensionless numbers like Reynolds (Re) and Prandtl (Pr).
  • In forced convection, empirical correlations relate Nu to Re and Pr, while in natural convection, Nu is linked to Gr and Pr.

Applications:

  • Heat exchangers: Used to predict performance and optimize fin or tube arrangements.
  • Electronics cooling: Determines whether forced convection is needed or natural convection suffices.
  • Industrial processing: Helps in reactor design, HVAC systems, and turbine cooling.
  • Biomedical devices: Critical in devices like thermal sensors and microfluidic systems.
Example Calculation

Calculating Nu for Water in a Pipe

h = 500 W/m²·K

L = 0.02 m

k = 0.6 W/m·K

Nu = (500 × 0.02) / 0.6 ≈ 16.67

This Nu ≈ 17 shows enhanced heat transfer due to convection.
