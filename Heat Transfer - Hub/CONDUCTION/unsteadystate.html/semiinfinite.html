üîπ Concept of Semi-Infinite Solids

A semi-infinite solid is an idealized approximation where the solid is assumed to be large in one direction (usually the depth) compared to the heat penetration depth. In this idealization, the thermal response of the solid at any point is primarily influenced by the surface condition, and it assumes no significant heat loss in the direction perpendicular to the surface.

Temperature Distribution with Constant Surface Temperature:

$$ \frac{T(x,t) - T_s}{T_i - T_s} = \text{erf} \left( \frac{x}{2\sqrt{\alpha t}} \right) $$

Where:

  • T(x,t) = Temperature at depth x and time t
  • T_s = Surface temperature
  • T_i = Initial temperature
  • Œ± = Thermal diffusivity
  • erf = Error function, representing the mathematical relation in heat diffusion

Where:

  • q‚ÇÄ = Surface heat flux
  • k = Thermal conductivity
  • x = Distance from the surface
  • t = Time
  • Œ± = Thermal diffusivity

Complementary Error Function:

$$ \text{erfc}(x) = 1 - \text{erf}(x) $$

  • erfc(x) = Complementary error function

üìö More Insights

The error function erf and complementary error function erfc are essential in solving unsteady-state heat transfer problems in semi-infinite solids. These mathematical functions express the spread of heat in the solid and provide a means to calculate the temperature at any depth as a function of time.

Consider a semi-infinite solid with an initial temperature Ti = 100¬∞C and a surface temperature Ts = 0¬∞C. For a given time t = 10 seconds and thermal diffusivity Œ± = 1.2 x 10-6 m¬≤/s, calculate the temperature at a depth x = 0.02 meters.

Using the temperature distribution equation for a constant surface temperature, substitute the values into the formula:

            $$ \frac{T(x,t) - T_s}{T_i - T_s} = \text{erf} \left( \frac{x}{2\sqrt{\alpha t}} \right) $$

Once the error function value is calculated, the temperature at that point can be determined. The complementary error function can also be used to determine the remaining heat distribution.

Semi-infinite solids and their temperature distribution models have real-world applications in several industries, including:
