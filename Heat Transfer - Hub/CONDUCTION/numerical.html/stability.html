üìò Introduction to Stability

In numerical simulations of unsteady heat conduction using finite difference methods, **stability** refers to the condition where numerical errors do not grow uncontrollably as the solution progresses in time.

Stability ensures that the computed temperature remains physically meaningful and does not diverge due to round-off or truncation errors.

üßÆ Fourier Number and Time Step Criterion

The **Fourier Number (Fo)** is a key parameter governing stability:

\[ Fo = \frac{\alpha \Delta t}{(\Delta x)^2} \]
  • \( \alpha \): Thermal diffusivity \((m^2/s)\)
  • \( \Delta t \): Time step
  • \( \Delta x \): Spatial step

For **explicit schemes**, a strict stability condition must be satisfied:

\[ Fo \leq \frac{1}{2} \]

Violation of this leads to instability and non-physical oscillations in temperature results.

‚öñÔ∏è Explicit vs Implicit Stability

  • Explicit Scheme: Requires very small time steps to maintain stability. Very fast but unstable for large \( \Delta t \).
  • Implicit Scheme: Unconditionally stable. You can use large \( \Delta t \) without numerical instability, but it requires solving a system of equations.

üìä Amplification Factor Analysis

Stability can also be analyzed by checking the **amplification factor** \( G \). For a stable solution, the magnitude must satisfy:

\[ |G| \leq 1 \]

If \( |G| > 1 \), numerical errors grow exponentially with time, leading to divergence.

üìê Practical Tips for Stable Simulation

  • Use a fine mesh in regions with steep gradients
  • Choose \( \Delta t \) based on stability limits for explicit methods
  • Use implicit methods for large time scales or long simulations
  • Validate results against analytical or benchmark data
