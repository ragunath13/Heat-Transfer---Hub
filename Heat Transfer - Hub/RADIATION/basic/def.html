Thermal Radiation: Definition and Physical Mechanism

Thermal radiation is the transfer of heat through electromagnetic waves. Unlike conduction and convection, it does not require a medium for propagation and can travel through a vacuum.

All matter with a temperature above absolute zero ($0 K$) emits thermal radiation, with the amount and spectral distribution of emitted energy depending on the object's temperature and surface properties.

The Physical Mechanism of Emission

Atomic and Molecular Oscillations

Thermal radiation is fundamentally caused by the acceleration of charges (electrons and protons) within atoms and molecules. As these particles oscillate due to their thermal energy, they emit electromagnetic waves.

The higher the temperature of a body, the more vigorous the atomic and molecular oscillations, and thus the greater the rate of radiation emission.

Thermal radiation spans a wide range of the electromagnetic spectrum, primarily in the infrared (IR) region, but also extending into the visible and ultraviolet (UV) regions at very high temperatures.

The wavelength at which maximum emission occurs shifts to shorter wavelengths as temperature increases (Wien's Displacement Law).

DID YOU KNOW? The Sun's surface temperature of approximately $5800 K$ causes it to emit most of its radiation in the visible light spectrum, which is why we can see it! Human bodies, at around $310 K$, primarily emit in the infrared range.
Key Characteristics of Thermal Radiation
  • No Medium Required: Can travel through a vacuum (e.g., from Sun to Earth).
  • Temperature Dependent: All objects above $0 K$ emit radiation, and emission rate increases with $T^4$ (Stefan-Boltzmann Law).
  • Electromagnetic Waves: Travels at the speed of light.
  • Surface Phenomenon: Emitted from the surface of a body.
  • Directional: Radiation can be emitted and absorbed directionally.
  • Spectral: Emitted at various wavelengths, forming a continuous spectrum.
