View Factor Relations for Common Geometries

Calculating view factors using the integral definition can be complex. Fortunately, for many common and simplified geometries, analytical solutions or established charts and tables exist. These pre-calculated relations are invaluable for engineers in various applications.

Understanding these standard configurations allows for quick and accurate estimation of radiative heat transfer between surfaces.

Example: Floors and Ceilings, Parallel Walls

For two parallel rectangular plates of equal size, $F_{1 \to 2}$ depends on dimensions and separation distance.

General formula for parallel plates with common side: $F_{1 \to 2} = f(X, Y)$

Where $X = L/D$ and $Y = W/D$ (L, W are dimensions, D is separation).

Example: Corners of a Room, L-shaped Ducts

For two perpendicular rectangular plates with a common edge, $F_{1 \to 2}$ is typically found using charts or complex algebraic expressions involving $H/L$ and $W/L$.

Formula for perpendicular plates: $F_{1 \to 2} = f(H/L, W/L)$

Example: Concentric Pipes, Annular Ducts

For coaxial cylinders, view factors depend on radii ($R_1, R_2$) and length ($L$).

For inner cylinder to outer cylinder: $F_{1 \to 2} = 1$ (if infinitely long or ends are closed).

If open ends, $F_{1 \to 2} < 1$, and radiation can escape through ends ($F_{1 \to \text{ends}}$).

DID YOU KNOW? For a small surface on a large sphere, the view factor from the small surface to the rest of the sphere is approximately 1, as almost all radiation from the small surface will hit the sphere!
Applications of Common View Factor Relations
  • Heat Exchanger Design: Calculating radiative heat transfer between internal surfaces of shell-and-tube or plate heat exchangers.
  • Furnace & Boiler Design: Estimating radiation exchange between flame, walls, and tubes.
  • Building Thermal Loads: Analyzing heat gain/loss through windows and walls due to radiation from adjacent surfaces.
  • Cryogenic Systems: Designing multi-layer insulation where radiation between parallel shields is critical.
  • Solar Concentrators: Calculating efficiency of collectors with various reflector and receiver geometries.
