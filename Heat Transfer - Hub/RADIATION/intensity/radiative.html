Radiative Intensity & Solid Angle

When analyzing thermal radiation, it's often not enough to know the total energy emitted from a surface. We need to understand how that energy is distributed in space. This is where the concepts of **Radiative Intensity** and **Solid Angle** become crucial.

These concepts allow us to quantify radiation flow in specific directions and over defined regions of space.

Solid Angle ($\omega$ or $\Omega$)

The **solid angle** is the three-dimensional equivalent of a planar angle. It describes how large an object appears from a certain point of view, or how much of the field of view a given object subtends.

It is defined as the ratio of the area of a spherical surface ($A$) subtended by the object to the square of the radius ($r$) of the sphere centered at the point of view.

The unit of solid angle is the **steradian (sr)**. A full sphere subtends a solid angle of $4\pi$ steradians.

For a small area $dA$ on a sphere at distance $r$ from the center, the solid angle $d\Omega$ is given by:

$d\Omega = \frac{dA}{r^2}$

Radiative Intensity ($I$)

The **radiative intensity** (or radiant intensity) is a measure of the power emitted, reflected, or transmitted by a surface per unit area per unit solid angle. It describes the strength of radiation in a specific direction.

It is a directional quantity, unlike total emissive power which is hemispherical. Its unit is typically W/(m$^2 \cdot sr$).

For a surface emitting power $dQ$ from an area $dA$ into a solid angle $d\Omega$ in a direction normal to the surface, the intensity is:

$I = \frac{dQ}{dA \cdot d\Omega}$

If the radiation is emitted at an angle $\theta$ to the surface normal, the projected area $dA \cos\theta$ is used:

$I = \frac{dQ}{dA \cos\theta \cdot d\Omega}$

Implications and Applications
  • Radiometer Design: Instruments that measure radiation (like pyrometers) are designed to collect radiation within a specific solid angle.
  • Light Source Characterization: Describing the directional output of LEDs, lasers, and other light sources.
  • Solar Concentrators: Designing mirrors and lenses to focus solar radiation from a large solid angle onto a smaller receiver.
  • Satellite Communication: Antennas are designed to transmit and receive signals within narrow solid angles for efficient communication.
  • Thermal Imaging: Understanding how much radiation is collected from different parts of a scene to form an image.
