Applications in Solar Collectors and Buildings

The principles of radiation and heat transfer are fundamental to the design and operation of solar collectors and energy-efficient buildings. By understanding how surfaces interact with solar radiation and how heat moves, we can effectively harness solar energy and minimize unwanted heat gain or loss.

This involves optimizing properties like absorptivity, emissivity, and reflectivity, as well as employing insulation strategies to control thermal flow.

Solar Collectors: Capturing the Sun's Energy
  • Photovoltaic (PV) Panels: Convert sunlight directly into electricity. Their surfaces are designed to have high solar absorptivity across the visible and near-infrared spectrum.
  • Solar Thermal Collectors: Absorb solar radiation to heat a fluid (water or air). Surfaces are typically dark and have high absorptivity to maximize heat capture. Selective coatings are used to have high absorptivity for solar wavelengths and low emissivity for thermal infrared wavelengths to prevent re-radiation.
  • Concentrating Solar Power (CSP): Use mirrors to focus sunlight onto a receiver. The receiver surface is designed for very high absorptivity.
  • Glazing: Transparent covers (glass) on collectors allow solar radiation to pass through but trap re-emitted long-wave infrared radiation, creating a greenhouse effect to enhance heat retention.
Buildings: Managing Thermal Comfort and Efficiency
  • Cool Roofs: Utilize highly reflective (high albedo) and high-emissivity materials to reflect sunlight and radiate absorbed heat away, reducing cooling loads in hot climates.
  • Low-Emissivity (Low-E) Windows: Have thin, transparent metallic coatings that reflect infrared radiation. In winter, they reflect internal heat back indoors; in summer, they reflect external heat outwards.
  • Passive Solar Design: Orienting buildings and using materials (e.g., thermal mass like concrete floors) to absorb solar heat during the day and release it slowly at night, reducing heating needs.
  • Insulation: Materials (fiberglass, foam) reduce conductive and convective heat transfer. Radiant barriers (reflective foils) are used in attics to block radiative heat transfer.
  • Daylighting: Strategic window placement and shading devices maximize natural light while minimizing solar heat gain.
