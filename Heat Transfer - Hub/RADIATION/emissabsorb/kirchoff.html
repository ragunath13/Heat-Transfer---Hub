Kirchhoff’s Law of Thermal Radiation

Kirchhoff's Law of Thermal Radiation states that for a body in thermal equilibrium with its surroundings, its emissivity ($\epsilon$) equals its absorptivity ($\alpha$) at any given wavelength and temperature.

In simpler terms, a good absorber of thermal radiation is also a good emitter of thermal radiation, and a poor absorber is a poor emitter.

The Mathematical Formulation

Kirchhoff's Law is expressed as:

$\epsilon_{\lambda,T} = \alpha_{\lambda,T}$

Where:

  • $\epsilon_{\lambda,T}$: Spectral emissivity at a specific wavelength ($\lambda$) and temperature ($T$).
  • $\alpha_{\lambda,T}$: Spectral absorptivity at the same wavelength ($\lambda$) and temperature ($T$).

This law applies to the spectral (wavelength-dependent) and directional properties of a surface when it is in thermal equilibrium.

Implications and Applications
  • Blackbody as Ideal: A perfect blackbody has $\epsilon=1$ and $\alpha=1$, confirming Kirchhoff's law for the ideal case.
  • Radiative Cooling: Surfaces designed for radiative cooling (e.g., cool roofs) are often white (low solar absorptivity) but have high emissivity in the infrared spectrum to efficiently radiate heat away.
  • Solar Collectors: Selective surfaces for solar collectors have high absorptivity for solar radiation (short wavelengths) and low emissivity for thermal radiation (long wavelengths) to minimize heat loss. This is a spectral application of the law.
  • Infrared Heaters: Materials used in infrared heaters are chosen for their high emissivity in the infrared range to efficiently radiate heat.
  • Temperature Measurement: Understanding this law is critical for accurate non-contact temperature measurements using pyrometers, as surface emissivity must be accounted for.
