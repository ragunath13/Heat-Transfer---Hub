Stefan–Boltzmann Law

The **Stefan–Boltzmann Law** describes the total energy radiated per unit surface area of a blackbody per unit time (its emissive power) is directly proportional to the fourth power of its absolute temperature.

This law is crucial for understanding the total radiant energy emitted by objects at various temperatures, from stars to industrial furnaces.

The Mathematical Formulation

The Stefan–Boltzmann Law is expressed as:

$E_b = \sigma T^4$

Where:

  • $E_b$: Total emissive power of a blackbody (W/m$^2$)
  • $\sigma$: Stefan–Boltzmann constant ($5.670 \times 10^{-8} \text{ W/(m}^2 \cdot K^4)$)
  • $T$: Absolute temperature of the blackbody (K)

For real surfaces, the total emissive power is given by $E = \epsilon \sigma T^4$, where $\epsilon$ is the emissivity of the surface ($0 \le \epsilon \le 1$).

Implications and Applications
  • Energy Balance: Crucial for calculating the net heat exchange between objects and their surroundings, especially at high temperatures.
  • Astrophysics: Used to estimate the luminosity and effective temperatures of stars and other celestial bodies.
  • Thermal Design: Essential for designing high-temperature equipment like furnaces, boilers, and spacecraft radiators.
  • Climate Science: Fundamental to understanding Earth's energy balance and the greenhouse effect (how Earth radiates heat back into space).
  • Pyrometry: Forms the basis for non-contact temperature measurement by measuring the total radiant power.
