Heat Transfer Problem Solving

Deepen your understanding with practical examples and clear formula breakdowns.

Conduction

Heat transfer through direct contact of particles.

Fourier's Law of Heat Conduction (Plane Wall)

This law describes the rate of heat transfer through a material by conduction. It states that the rate of heat flow is proportional to the negative temperature gradient and the area perpendicular to that gradient.

$$ Q = -k A \frac{dT}{dx} $$

Where:

  • $Q$ = Rate of heat transfer (Watts)
  • $k$ = Thermal conductivity of the material ($W/(m \cdot K)$)
  • $A$ = Cross-sectional area perpendicular to heat flow ($m^2$)
  • $\frac{dT}{dx}$ = Temperature gradient (change in temperature per unit length) ($K/m$)
  • The negative sign indicates heat flows from higher to lower temperature.

Problem Example: Heat Loss Through a Wall

Problem Statement:

A brick wall ($k = 0.7 \ W/(m \cdot K)$) is $0.2 \ m$ thick and has a cross-sectional area of $10 \ m^2$. If the inner surface is at $20^\circ C$ and the outer surface is at $5^\circ C$, calculate the rate of heat loss through the wall.

Solution:

  1. Identify Given Values:
    • $k = 0.7 \ W/(m \cdot K)$
    • $A = 10 \ m^2$
    • $dx = 0.2 \ m$
    • $T_{inner} = 20^\circ C = 293.15 \ K$
    • $T_{outer} = 5^\circ C = 278.15 \ K$
  2. Calculate Temperature Difference ($\Delta T$):

    $\Delta T = T_{outer} - T_{inner} = 5^\circ C - 20^\circ C = -15^\circ C$ (or $-15 \ K$)

  3. Apply Fourier's Law:

    $$ Q = -k A \frac{\Delta T}{dx} $$

    $$ Q = -(0.7 \ W/(m \cdot K)) \times (10 \ m^2) \times \frac{(-15 \ K)}{(0.2 \ m)} $$

    $$ Q = -(0.7 \times 10 \times -75) \ W $$

    $$ Q = 525 \ W $$

Result: The rate of heat loss through the wall is $525 \ W$.

Steady-State Conduction through a Cylinder

Heat transfer in cylindrical coordinates, common in pipes and tubes.

Heat Transfer Rate in a Cylinder

For steady-state heat conduction through a cylindrical wall (like a pipe), the heat transfer rate is given by a formula that accounts for the radial path of heat flow.

$$ Q = \frac{2 \pi k L (T_i - T_o)}{\ln(r_o/r_i)} $$

Where:

  • $Q$ = Rate of heat transfer (Watts)
  • $k$ = Thermal conductivity of the cylinder material ($W/(m \cdot K)$)
  • $L$ = Length of the cylinder (m)
  • $T_i$ = Inner surface temperature ($K$ or $^\circ C$)
  • $T_o$ = Outer surface temperature ($K$ or $^\circ C$)
  • $r_i$ = Inner radius (m)
  • $r_o$ = Outer radius (m)

Problem Example: Heat Loss from an Insulated Pipe

Problem Statement:

A steam pipe has an inner radius of $0.05 \ m$ and an outer radius of $0.06 \ m$. It is $10 \ m$ long and made of a material with thermal conductivity $k = 45 \ W/(m \cdot K)$. If the inner surface temperature is $120^\circ C$ and the outer surface temperature is $115^\circ C$, calculate the heat loss from the pipe.

Solution:

  1. Identify Given Values:
    • $r_i = 0.05 \ m$
    • $r_o = 0.06 \ m$
    • $L = 10 \ m$
    • $k = 45 \ W/(m \cdot K)$
    • $T_i = 120^\circ C$
    • $T_o = 115^\circ C$
  2. Apply Cylindrical Conduction Formula:

    $$ Q = \frac{2 \pi k L (T_i - T_o)}{\ln(r_o/r_i)} $$

    $$ Q = \frac{2 \pi (45 \ W/(m \cdot K)) (10 \ m) (120^\circ C - 115^\circ C)}{\ln(0.06 \ m / 0.05 \ m)} $$

    $$ Q = \frac{2 \pi (450) (5)}{\ln(1.2)} $$

    $$ Q = \frac{14137.167}{0.1823} \ W $$

    $$ Q \approx 77548 \ W \text{ or } 77.55 \ kW $$

Result: The heat loss from the pipe is approximately $77.55 \ kW$.

Critical Thickness of Insulation

Understanding when adding insulation can actually increase heat loss.

Critical Radius of Insulation

For cylindrical and spherical geometries, there exists a "critical radius" of insulation. Adding insulation up to this radius increases heat transfer, while adding beyond it decreases heat transfer. This is due to the combined effect of reduced conduction resistance and increased convection surface area.

$$ r_{crit} = \frac{k_{ins}}{h} \quad \text{(for cylinder)} $$

$$ r_{crit} = \frac{2 k_{ins}}{h} \quad \text{(for sphere)} $$

Where:

  • $r_{crit}$ = Critical radius of insulation (m)
  • $k_{ins}$ = Thermal conductivity of the insulation material ($W/(m \cdot K)$)
  • $h$ = Convection heat transfer coefficient from the outer surface of insulation to the ambient ($W/(m^2 \cdot K)$)

Problem Example: Critical Insulation for a Small Pipe

Problem Statement:

A small pipe has an outer radius of $0.01 \ m$. It is to be insulated with a material having a thermal conductivity $k_{ins} = 0.05 \ W/(m \cdot K)$. The convection heat transfer coefficient from the outer surface of the insulation to the surroundings is $h = 10 \ W/(m^2 \cdot K)$. Determine the critical radius of insulation for this pipe.

Solution:

  1. Identify Given Values:
    • $k_{ins} = 0.05 \ W/(m \cdot K)$
    • $h = 10 \ W/(m^2 \cdot K)$
  2. Apply Critical Radius Formula for a Cylinder:

    $$ r_{crit} = \frac{k_{ins}}{h} $$

    $$ r_{crit} = \frac{0.05 \ W/(m \cdot K)}{10 \ W/(m^2 \cdot K)} $$

    $$ r_{crit} = 0.005 \ m $$

Result: The critical radius of insulation for this pipe is $0.005 \ m$. Since the pipe's outer radius ($0.01 \ m$) is greater than the critical radius, adding insulation will always reduce heat loss in this case.

Overall Heat Transfer Coefficient (U-factor)

Describes the overall rate of heat transfer through a composite barrier.

Overall Heat Transfer Rate

When heat transfers through multiple layers (like a wall with insulation) and/or involves convection on both sides, the overall heat transfer coefficient ($U$) simplifies the calculation of the total heat transfer rate.

$$ Q = U A \Delta T_{overall} $$

Where:

  • $Q$ = Rate of heat transfer (Watts)
  • $U$ = Overall heat transfer coefficient ($W/(m^2 \cdot K)$)
  • $A$ = Total heat transfer area ($m^2$)
  • $\Delta T_{overall}$ = Overall temperature difference across the barrier ($K$ or $^\circ C$)

The overall heat transfer coefficient $U$ is related to individual resistances:

$$ \frac{1}{U A} = R_{total} = R_{conv,1} + R_{cond,1} + R_{cond,2} + \dots + R_{conv,2} $$

Problem Example: Heat Transfer Through a Composite Wall

Problem Statement:

A wall consists of a $0.02 \ m$ thick plasterboard ($k_1 = 0.17 \ W/(m \cdot K)$) and a $0.05 \ m$ thick insulation layer ($k_2 = 0.04 \ W/(m \cdot K)$). The convection coefficients on the inner and outer surfaces are $h_i = 10 \ W/(m^2 \cdot K)$ and $h_o = 25 \ W/(m^2 \cdot K)$, respectively. If the total area is $1 \ m^2$, calculate the overall heat transfer coefficient $U$ for the wall.

Solution:

  1. Calculate Individual Thermal Resistances:
    • Convection (inner): $R_{conv,i} = \frac{1}{h_i A} = \frac{1}{10 \times 1} = 0.1 \ K/W$
    • Conduction (plasterboard): $R_{cond,1} = \frac{L_1}{k_1 A} = \frac{0.02}{0.17 \times 1} \approx 0.1176 \ K/W$
    • Conduction (insulation): $R_{cond,2} = \frac{L_2}{k_2 A} = \frac{0.05}{0.04 \times 1} = 1.25 \ K/W$
    • Convection (outer): $R_{conv,o} = \frac{1}{h_o A} = \frac{1}{25 \times 1} = 0.04 \ K/W$
  2. Calculate Total Thermal Resistance ($R_{total}$):

    $$ R_{total} = R_{conv,i} + R_{cond,1} + R_{cond,2} + R_{conv,o} $$

    $$ R_{total} = 0.1 + 0.1176 + 1.25 + 0.04 = 1.5076 \ K/W $$

  3. Calculate Overall Heat Transfer Coefficient ($U$):

    Since $R_{total} = \frac{1}{U A}$, then $U = \frac{1}{R_{total} A}$

    $$ U = \frac{1}{1.5076 \times 1} \approx 0.663 \ W/(m^2 \cdot K) $$

Result: The overall heat transfer coefficient for the wall is approximately $0.663 \ W/(m^2 \cdot K)$.

Thermal Resistance

An analogy to electrical resistance, simplifying heat transfer analysis.

Concept of Thermal Resistance

Thermal resistance ($R$) is a measure of a material's ability to resist heat flow. It's particularly useful for analyzing heat transfer through composite walls or systems with multiple modes of heat transfer in series.

$$ R_{conduction} = \frac{L}{k A} $$

$$ R_{convection} = \frac{1}{h A} $$

Where:

  • $L$ = Thickness of the material (m)
  • $k$ = Thermal conductivity ($W/(m \cdot K)$)
  • $A$ = Heat transfer area ($m^2$)
  • $h$ = Convection heat transfer coefficient ($W/(m^2 \cdot K)$)

For resistances in series, $R_{total} = R_1 + R_2 + \dots$

For resistances in parallel, $\frac{1}{R_{total}} = \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} + \dots$

Problem Example: Heat Flow Through a Window

Problem Statement:

A single-pane window has a glass thickness of $0.004 \ m$ and a thermal conductivity of $0.78 \ W/(m \cdot K)$. The window area is $1.2 \ m^2$. Calculate the thermal resistance of the glass pane to heat conduction.

Solution:

  1. Identify Given Values:
    • $L = 0.004 \ m$
    • $k = 0.78 \ W/(m \cdot K)$
    • $A = 1.2 \ m^2$
  2. Apply Conduction Thermal Resistance Formula:

    $$ R_{conduction} = \frac{L}{k A} $$

    $$ R_{conduction} = \frac{0.004 \ m}{(0.78 \ W/(m \cdot K)) \times (1.2 \ m^2)} $$

    $$ R_{conduction} = \frac{0.004}{0.936} \ K/W $$

    $$ R_{conduction} \approx 0.00427 \ K/W $$

Result: The thermal resistance of the glass pane is approximately $0.00427 \ K/W$.

Convection (External Flow)

Heat transfer through fluid motion (liquids or gases).

Newton's Law of Cooling

This law describes the rate of heat transfer between a solid surface and a moving fluid. It's often used for both natural (free) and forced convection.

$$ Q = h A (T_s - T_\infty) $$

Where:

  • $Q$ = Rate of heat transfer (Watts)
  • $h$ = Convection heat transfer coefficient ($W/(m^2 \cdot K)$)
  • $A$ = Surface area of heat transfer ($m^2$)
  • $T_s$ = Surface temperature ($K$ or $^\circ C$)
  • $T_\infty$ = Fluid temperature far from the surface ($K$ or $^\circ C$)

Problem Example: Cooling of a Hot Plate

Problem Statement:

A flat plate with a surface area of $0.5 \ m^2$ is at a temperature of $100^\circ C$. Air at $25^\circ C$ flows over the plate, and the convection heat transfer coefficient is $15 \ W/(m^2 \cdot K)$. Calculate the rate of heat loss from the plate to the air.

Solution:

  1. Identify Given Values:
    • $h = 15 \ W/(m^2 \cdot K)$
    • $A = 0.5 \ m^2$
    • $T_s = 100^\circ C$
    • $T_\infty = 25^\circ C$
  2. Calculate Temperature Difference ($\Delta T$):

    $\Delta T = T_s - T_\infty = 100^\circ C - 25^\circ C = 75^\circ C$ (or $75 \ K$)

  3. Apply Newton's Law of Cooling:

    $$ Q = h A (T_s - T_\infty) $$

    $$ Q = (15 \ W/(m^2 \cdot K)) \times (0.5 \ m^2) \times (75 \ K) $$

    $$ Q = 7.5 \times 75 \ W $$

    $$ Q = 562.5 \ W $$

Result: The rate of heat loss from the plate to the air is $562.5 \ W$.

Forced Convection over a Flat Plate (Laminar)

Analyzing heat transfer when fluid flows over a surface.

Local Nusselt Number for Laminar Flow

For laminar flow over a flat plate (Reynolds number $Re_x < 5 \times 10^5$), the local Nusselt number can be used to find the local convection heat transfer coefficient.

$$ Nu_x = \frac{h_x x}{k_f} = 0.338 Re_x^{0.5} Pr^{1/3} $$

Where:

  • $Nu_x$ = Local Nusselt number (dimensionless)
  • $h_x$ = Local convection heat transfer coefficient ($W/(m^2 \cdot K)$)
  • $x$ = Distance from the leading edge (m)
  • $k_f$ = Thermal conductivity of the fluid ($W/(m \cdot K)$)
  • $Re_x = \frac{\rho V x}{\mu}$ = Local Reynolds number (dimensionless)
  • $Pr = \frac{\mu c_p}{k_f}$ = Prandtl number (dimensionless)
  • $\rho$ = Fluid density ($kg/m^3$)
  • $V$ = Free stream velocity (m/s)
  • $\mu$ = Dynamic viscosity of the fluid ($N \cdot s/m^2$)
  • $c_p$ = Specific heat capacity of the fluid ($J/(kg \cdot K)$)

For average heat transfer coefficient over length L: $Nu_L = 2 \times Nu_x$ at $x=L$.

Problem Example: Air Flow Over a Plate

Problem Statement:

Air at $20^\circ C$ and $1 \ \text{atm}$ flows over a flat plate with a velocity of $2 \ m/s$. Determine the local convection heat transfer coefficient at a distance of $0.2 \ m$ from the leading edge. Assume properties of air at $20^\circ C$: $\rho = 1.204 \ kg/m^3$, $\mu = 1.825 \times 10^{-5} \ N \cdot s/m^2$, $k_f = 0.0251 \ W/(m \cdot K)$, $Pr = 0.731$.

Solution:

  1. Identify Given Values and Fluid Properties:
    • $V = 2 \ m/s$
    • $x = 0.2 \ m$
    • $\rho = 1.204 \ kg/m^3$
    • $\mu = 1.825 \times 10^{-5} \ N \cdot s/m^2$
    • $k_f = 0.0251 \ W/(m \cdot K)$
    • $Pr = 0.731$
  2. Calculate Local Reynolds Number ($Re_x$):

    $$ Re_x = \frac{\rho V x}{\mu} = \frac{(1.204 \ kg/m^3) (2 \ m/s) (0.2 \ m)}{1.825 \times 10^{-5} \ N \cdot s/m^2} $$

    $$ Re_x \approx 26361 $$

    Since $Re_x < 5 \times 10^5$, flow is laminar.

  3. Calculate Local Nusselt Number ($Nu_x$):

    $$ Nu_x = 0.338 Re_x^{0.5} Pr^{1/3} $$

    $$ Nu_x = 0.338 (26361)^{0.5} (0.731)^{1/3} $$

    $$ Nu_x = 0.338 (162.36) (0.899) $$

    $$ Nu_x \approx 49.26 $$

  4. Calculate Local Convection Heat Transfer Coefficient ($h_x$):

    $$ h_x = \frac{Nu_x k_f}{x} = \frac{(49.26) (0.0251 \ W/(m \cdot K))}{0.2 \ m} $$

    $$ h_x \approx 6.18 \ W/(m^2 \cdot K) $$

Result: The local convection heat transfer coefficient at $0.2 \ m$ is approximately $6.18 \ W/(m^2 \cdot K)$.

Natural Convection from a Vertical Plate

Heat transfer driven by density differences in a fluid due to temperature variations.

Nusselt Number for Natural Convection

For natural convection, the Grashof number ($Gr$) and Prandtl number ($Pr$) are combined into the Rayleigh number ($Ra$). For laminar natural convection over a vertical plate ($10^4 < Ra_L < 10^9$), a common correlation for the average Nusselt number is:

$$ Nu_L = \frac{h L}{k_f} = C (Gr_L Pr)^{n} = C Ra_L^n $$

Where for vertical plates (laminar): $C = 0.59$, $n = 1/4$

  • $Nu_L$ = Average Nusselt number (dimensionless)
  • $h$ = Average convection heat transfer coefficient ($W/(m^2 \cdot K)$)
  • $L$ = Characteristic length (height of the plate) (m)
  • $k_f$ = Thermal conductivity of the fluid ($W/(m \cdot K)$)
  • $Gr_L = \frac{g \beta (T_s - T_\infty) L^3}{\nu^2}$ = Grashof number (dimensionless)
  • $Pr = \frac{\nu}{\alpha}$ = Prandtl number (dimensionless)
  • $Ra_L = Gr_L Pr$ = Rayleigh number (dimensionless)
  • $g$ = Gravitational acceleration ($9.81 \ m/s^2$)
  • $\beta$ = Coefficient of thermal expansion ($1/K$) (for ideal gases, $\beta = 1/T_{film}$ in Kelvin)
  • $T_s$ = Surface temperature ($K$ or $^\circ C$)
  • $T_\infty$ = Ambient fluid temperature ($K$ or $^\circ C$)
  • $\nu$ = Kinematic viscosity of the fluid ($m^2/s$)
  • $\alpha$ = Thermal diffusivity of the fluid ($m^2/s$)

Problem Example: Heat Loss from a Vertical Plate

Problem Statement:

A vertical plate $0.5 \ m$ high is maintained at $90^\circ C$ in quiescent air at $20^\circ C$. Calculate the average heat transfer coefficient. Assume air properties at film temperature ($T_{film} = (90+20)/2 = 55^\circ C \approx 328 \ K$): $k_f = 0.0283 \ W/(m \cdot K)$, $\nu = 1.84 \times 10^{-5} \ m^2/s$, $Pr = 0.722$.

Solution:

  1. Identify Given Values and Fluid Properties:
    • $L = 0.5 \ m$
    • $T_s = 90^\circ C$
    • $T_\infty = 20^\circ C$
    • $T_{film} = 328 \ K \implies \beta = 1/328 \ K^{-1}$
    • $k_f = 0.0283 \ W/(m \cdot K)$
    • $\nu = 1.84 \times 10^{-5} \ m^2/s$
    • $Pr = 0.722$
  2. Calculate Grashof Number ($Gr_L$):

    $$ Gr_L = \frac{g \beta (T_s - T_\infty) L^3}{\nu^2} $$

    $$ Gr_L = \frac{(9.81 \ m/s^2) (1/328 \ K^{-1}) (90 - 20) \ K (0.5 \ m)^3}{(1.84 \times 10^{-5} \ m^2/s)^2} $$

    $$ Gr_L = \frac{9.81 \times (1/328) \times 70 \times 0.125}{3.3856 \times 10^{-10}} $$

    $$ Gr_L \approx 7.76 \times 10^7 $$

  3. Calculate Rayleigh Number ($Ra_L$):

    $$ Ra_L = Gr_L Pr = (7.76 \times 10^7) (0.722) $$

    $$ Ra_L \approx 5.60 \times 10^7 $$

    This falls within the laminar range ($10^4 < Ra_L < 10^9$).

  4. Calculate Average Nusselt Number ($Nu_L$):

    $$ Nu_L = 0.59 Ra_L^{1/4} = 0.59 (5.60 \times 10^7)^{1/4} $$

    $$ Nu_L = 0.59 (48.65) $$

    $$ Nu_L \approx 28.71 $$

  5. Calculate Average Convection Heat Transfer Coefficient ($h$):

    $$ h = \frac{Nu_L k_f}{L} = \frac{(28.71) (0.0283 \ W/(m \cdot K))}{0.5 \ m} $$

    $$ h \approx 1.625 \ W/(m^2 \cdot K) $$

Result: The average heat transfer coefficient is approximately $1.625 \ W/(m^2 \cdot K)$.

Heat Exchangers (Log Mean Temperature Difference - LMTD)

Analyzing heat transfer in devices designed for efficient energy exchange between fluids.

Heat Transfer Rate in Heat Exchangers

The rate of heat transfer in a heat exchanger is typically calculated using the overall heat transfer coefficient ($U$), the heat transfer surface area ($A$), and the Log Mean Temperature Difference ($\Delta T_{lm}$).

$$ Q = U A \Delta T_{lm} $$

Where:

  • $Q$ = Rate of heat transfer (Watts)
  • $U$ = Overall heat transfer coefficient ($W/(m^2 \cdot K)$)
  • $A$ = Heat transfer area ($m^2$)
  • $\Delta T_{lm}$ = Log Mean Temperature Difference ($K$ or $^\circ C$)

For a counter-flow heat exchanger:

$$ \Delta T_{lm} = \frac{\Delta T_1 - \Delta T_2}{\ln(\Delta T_1 / \Delta T_2)} $$

Where:

  • $\Delta T_1 = T_{h,in} - T_{c,out}$
  • $\Delta T_2 = T_{h,out} - T_{c,in}$
  • $T_{h,in}, T_{h,out}$ = Inlet/outlet temperatures of hot fluid
  • $T_{c,in}, T_{c,out}$ = Inlet/outlet temperatures of cold fluid

Problem Example: Counter-Flow Heat Exchanger

Problem Statement:

A counter-flow heat exchanger is used to cool $0.5 \ kg/s$ of hot oil ($c_p = 2.2 \ kJ/(kg \cdot K)$) from $100^\circ C$ to $60^\circ C$. Cooling water ($c_p = 4.18 \ kJ/(kg \cdot K)$) enters at $20^\circ C$ and leaves at $40^\circ C$. The overall heat transfer coefficient is $300 \ W/(m^2 \cdot K)$. Calculate the required heat transfer area.

Solution:

  1. Calculate Heat Transfer Rate ($Q$):

    Using hot oil side: $Q = \dot{m}_{oil} c_{p,oil} (T_{h,in} - T_{h,out})$

    $$ Q = (0.5 \ kg/s) (2.2 \times 10^3 \ J/(kg \cdot K)) (100 - 60) \ K $$

    $$ Q = 0.5 \times 2200 \times 40 = 44000 \ W = 44 \ kW $$

  2. Calculate Log Mean Temperature Differences:

    $$ \Delta T_1 = T_{h,in} - T_{c,out} = 100^\circ C - 40^\circ C = 60^\circ C $$

    $$ \Delta T_2 = T_{h,out} - T_{c,in} = 60^\circ C - 20^\circ C = 40^\circ C $$

    $$ \Delta T_{lm} = \frac{\Delta T_1 - \Delta T_2}{\ln(\Delta T_1 / \Delta T_2)} = \frac{60 - 40}{\ln(60 / 40)} = \frac{20}{\ln(1.5)} $$

    $$ \Delta T_{lm} = \frac{20}{0.40546} \approx 49.32^\circ C $$

  3. Calculate Required Area ($A$):

    $$ Q = U A \Delta T_{lm} \implies A = \frac{Q}{U \Delta T_{lm}} $$

    $$ A = \frac{44000 \ W}{(300 \ W/(m^2 \cdot K)) (49.32 \ K)} $$

    $$ A = \frac{44000}{14796} \approx 2.974 \ m^2 $$

Result: The required heat transfer area for the heat exchanger is approximately $2.974 \ m^2$.

Radiation (Blackbody and Graybody)

Heat transfer through electromagnetic waves.

Stefan-Boltzmann Law

This law describes the total energy radiated per unit surface area of a black body across all wavelengths per unit time. For real objects (gray bodies), emissivity is introduced.

$$ Q = \epsilon \sigma A (T_s^4 - T_{sur}^4) $$

Where:

  • $Q$ = Rate of heat transfer (Watts)
  • $\epsilon$ = Emissivity of the surface (dimensionless, $0 \le \epsilon \le 1$)
  • $\sigma$ = Stefan-Boltzmann constant ($5.67 \times 10^{-8} \ W/(m^2 \cdot K^4)$)
  • $A$ = Surface area of heat transfer ($m^2$)
  • $T_s$ = Absolute temperature of the surface (Kelvin)
  • $T_{sur}$ = Absolute temperature of the surroundings (Kelvin)

Problem Example: Radiant Heat from a Sphere

Problem Statement:

A spherical object with a surface area of $0.2 \ m^2$ and an emissivity of $0.8$ is maintained at $300^\circ C$. It is placed in a large room where the walls are at $25^\circ C$. Calculate the net rate of heat transfer by radiation from the sphere to the room.

Solution:

  1. Identify Given Values:
    • $\epsilon = 0.8$
    • $\sigma = 5.67 \times 10^{-8} \ W/(m^2 \cdot K^4)$
    • $A = 0.2 \ m^2$
    • $T_s = 300^\circ C = 300 + 273.15 = 573.15 \ K$
    • $T_{sur} = 25^\circ C = 25 + 273.15 = 298.15 \ K$
  2. Apply Stefan-Boltzmann Law:

    $$ Q = \epsilon \sigma A (T_s^4 - T_{sur}^4) $$

    $$ Q = (0.8) \times (5.67 \times 10^{-8}) \times (0.2) \times ((573.15)^4 - (298.15)^4) $$

    $$ Q = (0.8) \times (5.67 \times 10^{-8}) \times (0.2) \times (1.076 \times 10^{11} - 7.91 \times 10^9) $$

    $$ Q = (0.8) \times (5.67 \times 10^{-8}) \times (0.2) \times (9.969 \times 10^{10}) $$

    $$ Q \approx 903.6 \ W $$

Result: The net rate of heat transfer by radiation is approximately $903.6 \ W$.

View Factor (Shape Factor)

Quantifying the fraction of radiation leaving one surface that strikes another.

Definition and Reciprocity Rule

The view factor $F_{ij}$ is defined as the fraction of the radiation leaving surface $i$ that is intercepted by surface $j$. It plays a crucial role in radiation heat exchange between multiple surfaces.

$$ F_{ij} = \frac{\text{Radiation leaving } i \text{ and incident on } j}{\text{Total radiation leaving } i} $$

Reciprocity Rule:

$$ A_i F_{ij} = A_j F_{ji} $$

Summation Rule (for an enclosure):

$$ \sum_{j=1}^{N} F_{ij} = 1 $$

Problem Example: View Factor Between Parallel Plates

Problem Statement:

Consider two parallel, directly opposed, black plates of equal area ($A_1 = A_2 = 1 \ m^2$). If the view factor from surface 1 to surface 2 is $F_{12} = 0.8$, what is the view factor $F_{21}$ and the view factor from surface 1 to itself ($F_{11}$)?

Solution:

  1. Identify Given Values:
    • $A_1 = 1 \ m^2$
    • $A_2 = 1 \ m^2$
    • $F_{12} = 0.8$
  2. Calculate $F_{21}$ using Reciprocity Rule:

    $$ A_1 F_{12} = A_2 F_{21} $$

    $$ (1 \ m^2)(0.8) = (1 \ m^2) F_{21} $$

    $$ F_{21} = 0.8 $$

  3. Calculate $F_{11}$ using Summation Rule (assuming a two-surface enclosure):

    For a two-surface enclosure (surfaces 1 and 2), the summation rule for surface 1 is:

    $$ F_{11} + F_{12} = 1 $$

    Since $F_{12} = 0.8$:

    $$ F_{11} + 0.8 = 1 $$

    $$ F_{11} = 1 - 0.8 = 0.2 $$

Result: The view factor $F_{21}$ is $0.8$, and the view factor $F_{11}$ (from surface 1 to itself) is $0.2$.

Radiation Exchange Between Two Large Parallel Plates

Calculating net radiation heat transfer between two surfaces that "see" only each other.

Net Radiation Heat Exchange

For two large, parallel, diffuse-gray surfaces, the net radiation heat exchange is simplified because their view factors are $F_{12} = F_{21} = 1$.

$$ Q_{12} = \frac{\sigma A (T_1^4 - T_2^4)}{\frac{1}{\epsilon_1} + \frac{1}{\epsilon_2} - 1} $$

Where:

  • $Q_{12}$ = Net radiation heat transfer from surface 1 to surface 2 (Watts)
  • $\sigma$ = Stefan-Boltzmann constant ($5.67 \times 10^{-8} \ W/(m^2 \cdot K^4)$)
  • $A$ = Area of either surface ($m^2$)
  • $T_1, T_2$ = Absolute temperatures of surfaces 1 and 2 (Kelvin)
  • $\epsilon_1, \epsilon_2$ = Emissivities of surfaces 1 and 2 (dimensionless)

Problem Example: Heat Transfer Between Two Walls

Problem Statement:

Two large parallel plates, each $5 \ m^2$ in area, are separated by a small distance. Plate 1 is at $400^\circ C$ with an emissivity of $0.7$, and Plate 2 is at $100^\circ C$ with an emissivity of $0.5$. Calculate the net radiation heat exchange between them.

Solution:

  1. Identify Given Values:
    • $A = 5 \ m^2$
    • $T_1 = 400^\circ C = 673.15 \ K$
    • $\epsilon_1 = 0.7$
    • $T_2 = 100^\circ C = 373.15 \ K$
    • $\epsilon_2 = 0.5$
    • $\sigma = 5.67 \times 10^{-8} \ W/(m^2 \cdot K^4)$
  2. Apply Radiation Exchange Formula for Parallel Plates:

    $$ Q_{12} = \frac{\sigma A (T_1^4 - T_2^4)}{\frac{1}{\epsilon_1} + \frac{1}{\epsilon_2} - 1} $$

    $$ Q_{12} = \frac{(5.67 \times 10^{-8}) (5) ((673.15)^4 - (373.15)^4)}{\frac{1}{0.7} + \frac{1}{0.5} - 1} $$

    Numerator: $(5.67 \times 10^{-8}) (5) (2.062 \times 10^{11} - 1.939 \times 10^{10}) = (2.835 \times 10^{-7}) (1.8681 \times 10^{11}) \approx 52936 \ W$

    Denominator: $1.4286 + 2 - 1 = 2.4286$

    $$ Q_{12} = \frac{52936}{2.4286} \approx 21797 \ W $$

Result: The net radiation heat exchange between the two large parallel plates is approximately $21.8 \ kW$.

Radiation Shields

Reducing radiation heat transfer between surfaces using intermediate layers.

Effect of a Single Radiation Shield

A radiation shield is a thin, highly reflective (low emissivity) layer placed between two radiating surfaces to reduce the net radiation heat transfer between them. For two large parallel plates with one shield between them:

$$ Q_{12, \text{with shield}} = \frac{\sigma A (T_1^4 - T_2^4)}{\left(\frac{1}{\epsilon_1} + \frac{1}{\epsilon_s} - 1\right) + \left(\frac{1}{\epsilon_s} + \frac{1}{\epsilon_2} - 1\right)} $$

Where:

  • $Q_{12, \text{with shield}}$ = Net radiation heat transfer with a shield (Watts)
  • $\epsilon_s$ = Emissivity of the radiation shield
  • Other variables are as defined for parallel plates.

Alternatively, if $\epsilon_1 = \epsilon_2 = \epsilon_s = \epsilon$ (all emissivities are equal), then:

$$ Q_{12, \text{with shield}} = \frac{1}{2-\epsilon} \sigma A (T_1^4 - T_2^4) $$

Compared to no shield: $Q_{12, \text{no shield}} = \frac{\epsilon}{2-\epsilon} \sigma A (T_1^4 - T_2^4)$. So, for equal emissivities, a single shield reduces heat transfer by half.

Problem Example: Radiation Shield Between Plates

Problem Statement:

Two large parallel plates are at $T_1 = 500 \ K$ and $T_2 = 300 \ K$. Both plates have an emissivity of $\epsilon = 0.5$. A radiation shield with an emissivity of $\epsilon_s = 0.05$ is placed between them. The area is $1 \ m^2$. Calculate the percentage reduction in radiation heat transfer due to the shield.

Solution:

  1. Identify Given Values:
    • $T_1 = 500 \ K$
    • $T_2 = 300 \ K$
    • $\epsilon_1 = \epsilon_2 = 0.5$
    • $\epsilon_s = 0.05$
    • $A = 1 \ m^2$
    • $\sigma = 5.67 \times 10^{-8} \ W/(m^2 \cdot K^4)$
  2. Calculate Heat Transfer without Shield ($Q_{\text{no shield}}$):

    $$ Q_{\text{no shield}} = \frac{\sigma A (T_1^4 - T_2^4)}{\frac{1}{\epsilon_1} + \frac{1}{\epsilon_2} - 1} $$

    $$ Q_{\text{no shield}} = \frac{(5.67 \times 10^{-8}) (1) ((500)^4 - (300)^4)}{\frac{1}{0.5} + \frac{1}{0.5} - 1} $$

    Numerator: $(5.67 \times 10^{-8}) (1) (6.25 \times 10^{10} - 8.1 \times 10^9) = (5.67 \times 10^{-8}) (5.44 \times 10^{10}) \approx 3084.48 \ W$

    Denominator: $2 + 2 - 1 = 3$

    $$ Q_{\text{no shield}} = \frac{3084.48}{3} \approx 1028.16 \ W $$

  3. Calculate Heat Transfer with Shield ($Q_{\text{with shield}}$):

    $$ Q_{\text{with shield}} = \frac{\sigma A (T_1^4 - T_2^4)}{\left(\frac{1}{\epsilon_1} + \frac{1}{\epsilon_s} - 1\right) + \left(\frac{1}{\epsilon_s} + \frac{1}{\epsilon_2} - 1\right)} $$

    Denominator: $(\frac{1}{0.5} + \frac{1}{0.05} - 1) + (\frac{1}{0.05} + \frac{1}{0.5} - 1)$

    $= (2 + 20 - 1) + (20 + 2 - 1) = 21 + 21 = 42$

    $$ Q_{\text{with shield}} = \frac{3084.48}{42} \approx 73.44 \ W $$

  4. Calculate Percentage Reduction:

    $$ \text{Reduction} = \frac{Q_{\text{no shield}} - Q_{\text{with shield}}}{Q_{\text{no shield}}} \times 100\% $$

    $$ \text{Reduction} = \frac{1028.16 - 73.44}{1028.16} \times 100\% $$

    $$ \text{Reduction} = \frac{954.72}{1028.16} \times 100\% \approx 92.86\% $$

Result: The radiation heat transfer is reduced by approximately $92.86\%$ due to the single radiation shield.

Lumped Capacitance Method

Analyzing transient heat transfer in objects with uniform temperature.

Temperature Change with Time

The lumped capacitance method is used when the temperature within an object remains nearly uniform during a transient heating or cooling process. This is valid when the Biot number ($Bi$) is less than $0.1$.

$$ \frac{T(t) - T_\infty}{T_i - T_\infty} = e^{-\frac{h A_s}{\rho V c_p} t} $$

Where:

  • $T(t)$ = Temperature of the object at time $t$ ($K$ or $^\circ C$)
  • $T_\infty$ = Temperature of the surrounding fluid ($K$ or $^\circ C$)
  • $T_i$ = Initial temperature of the object ($K$ or $^\circ C$)
  • $h$ = Convection heat transfer coefficient ($W/(m^2 \cdot K)$)
  • $A_s$ = Surface area for convection ($m^2$)
  • $\rho$ = Density of the object ($kg/m^3$)
  • $V$ = Volume of the object ($m^3$)
  • $c_p$ = Specific heat capacity of the object ($J/(kg \cdot K)$)
  • $t$ = Time (seconds)

Problem Example: Cooling of a Copper Ball

Problem Statement:

A copper ball ($c_p = 385 \ J/(kg \cdot K)$, $\rho = 8933 \ kg/m^3$) with a diameter of $0.03 \ m$ is initially at $150^\circ C$. It is suddenly exposed to air at $25^\circ C$ with a convection heat transfer coefficient of $20 \ W/(m^2 \cdot K)$. Assuming the lumped capacitance method is applicable, determine the temperature of the ball after $5$ minutes.

Solution:

  1. Identify Given Values and Calculate Properties:
    • $D = 0.03 \ m \implies R = 0.015 \ m$
    • $V = \frac{4}{3} \pi R^3 = \frac{4}{3} \pi (0.015)^3 \approx 1.4137 \times 10^{-5} \ m^3$
    • $A_s = 4 \pi R^2 = 4 \pi (0.015)^2 \approx 2.8274 \times 10^{-3} \ m^2$
    • $c_p = 385 \ J/(kg \cdot K)$
    • $\rho = 8933 \ kg/m^3$
    • $T_i = 150^\circ C$
    • $T_\infty = 25^\circ C$
    • $h = 20 \ W/(m^2 \cdot K)$
    • $t = 5 \ \text{minutes} = 300 \ \text{seconds}$
  2. Calculate the exponent term:

    $$ \frac{h A_s}{\rho V c_p} = \frac{20 \times 2.8274 \times 10^{-3}}{8933 \times 1.4137 \times 10^{-5} \times 385} $$

    $$ = \frac{0.056548}{48.64} \approx 0.0011625 \ s^{-1} $$

  3. Apply Lumped Capacitance Equation:

    $$ \frac{T(t) - T_\infty}{T_i - T_\infty} = e^{-\left(\frac{h A_s}{\rho V c_p}\right) t} $$

    $$ \frac{T(t) - 25}{150 - 25} = e^{-(0.0011625) \times 300} $$

    $$ \frac{T(t) - 25}{125} = e^{-0.34875} $$

    $$ \frac{T(t) - 25}{125} \approx 0.7054 $$

    $$ T(t) - 25 = 125 \times 0.7054 $$

    $$ T(t) - 25 = 88.175 $$

    $$ T(t) = 88.175 + 25 $$

    $$ T(t) \approx 113.18^\circ C $$

Result: The temperature of the copper ball after $5$ minutes is approximately $113.18^\circ C$.
