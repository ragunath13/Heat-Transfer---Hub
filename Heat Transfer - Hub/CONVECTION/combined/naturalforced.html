Interaction of Natural & Forced Convection

When buoyancy and mechanical forces combine in heat transfer

Natural Convection

Buoyancy-Driven:

  • Density differences create flow
  • Grashof number (Gr) dominant
  • Vertical flows predominant
  • Common in geophysical flows

Forced Convection

Mechanically-Driven:

  • External forcing creates flow
  • Reynolds number (Re) dominant
  • Direction controlled by geometry
  • Common in engineering systems

Mixed Convection

Interaction Effects:

  • Both Gr and Re significant (0.1 < Gr/Re² < 10)
  • Flow direction affects heat transfer
  • Can enhance or suppress convection
  • Richardson number (Ri = Gr/Re²) characterizes regime

Key Concept: Competing Mechanisms

Mixed convection occurs when both natural and forced convection mechanisms contribute significantly to heat transfer. The interaction can be:

  • Assisting: When forced flow aligns with natural convection (e.g., upward flow in heated pipe)
  • Opposing: When forced flow works against natural convection (e.g., downward flow in heated pipe)
  • Transverse: When forced flow is perpendicular to buoyancy direction
Flow Regimes and Transitions
Richardson Number (Ri) Regime Characteristics
Ri < 0.1 Forced convection dominant Buoyancy effects negligible
0.1 ≤ Ri ≤ 10 Mixed convection Both mechanisms significant
Ri > 10 Natural convection dominant Forced flow effects negligible
Governing Parameters

Grashof Number (Gr)

Buoyancy to viscous force ratio

Gr = (g·β·ΔT·L³)/ν²

Reynolds Number (Re)

Inertial to viscous force ratio

Re = (ρ·v·L)/μ

Richardson Number (Ri)

Buoyancy to inertial force ratio

Ri = Gr/Re²

Example: Building Ventilation

Combination of natural and forced convection in action:
• Stack effect creates natural airflow (temperature difference)
• Wind creates forced convection on building exterior
• HVAC systems add controlled forced convection
• Result is complex mixed convection throughout structure

Understanding these interactions is crucial for energy-efficient building design!
