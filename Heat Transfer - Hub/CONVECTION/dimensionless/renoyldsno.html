Reynolds Number in Convection

What is Reynolds Number?

The Reynolds Number (Re) is a dimensionless quantity that predicts flow patterns in fluid dynamics:

Re = (ρ·v·L)/μ

ρ: Fluid density (kg/m³) v: Velocity (m/s) L: Characteristic length (m) μ: Dynamic viscosity (Pa·s)

Physical Significance:

  • Ratio of inertial forces to viscous forces
  • Predicts transition between laminar and turbulent flow
  • Critical for determining heat transfer coefficients
Flow Regimes
Laminar
Re < 2300
Transitional
2300 < Re < 4000
Turbulent
Re > 4000

Laminar Flow (Re < 2300)

  • Smooth, orderly fluid motion
  • Parallel layers with minimal mixing
  • Lower heat transfer rates
  • Primary heat transfer mechanism: conduction

Transitional Flow (2300 < Re < 4000)

  • Unstable flow conditions
  • Intermittent turbulent bursts
  • Difficult to predict behavior
  • Varying heat transfer rates

Turbulent Flow (Re > 4000)

  • Chaotic, mixing fluid motion
  • Enhanced heat transfer
  • Higher pressure drops
  • Primary heat transfer mechanism: eddy diffusion
Reynolds Number in Heat Transfer

Relationship to Convective Heat Transfer

The Reynolds Number directly influences the Nusselt Number (Nu) and thus the convective heat transfer coefficient:

Nu = C·Rem·Prn

Where Pr is the Prandtl number, and C, m, n are constants depending on geometry and flow conditions.

Practical Implications:

  • Turbulent flow typically provides 5-10x higher heat transfer than laminar flow
  • Engineers often design systems to operate in the turbulent regime for better heat transfer
  • Higher Re requires more pumping power due to increased friction
Calculating Reynolds Number

Example Calculation

For water flowing through a 2cm diameter pipe at 1 m/s:

ρ = 1000 kg/m³

v = 1 m/s

L = 0.02 m (pipe diameter)

μ = 0.001 Pa·s

Re = (1000 × 1 × 0.02) / 0.001 = 20,000

This Re = 20,000 indicates turbulent flow.
