Grashof Number in Natural Convection

What is Grashof Number?

The Grashof Number (Gr) is a dimensionless number that helps us understand natural convection. It measures the ratio of the buoyancy forces to the viscous forces in a fluid. This number tells us how fluid will move when it is heated and why convection occurs in many natural systems, like the air around a heated object or the rising of hot air in a room.

Gr = [g·β·(Ts - T∞)·L³] / ν²

g: Acceleration due to gravity (m/s²) β: Thermal expansion coefficient (1/K) Ts - T∞: Temperature difference (K) L: Characteristic length (m) ν: Kinematic viscosity (m²/s)

Physical Significance:

  • The Grashof Number compares buoyancy forces (upward forces due to heated fluid) to viscous forces (resistance to fluid flow due to viscosity).
  • It determines the onset of natural convection in a system.
  • If the Grashof number is higher, it means there will be stronger convection currents, which results in more heat transfer.

Why is Grashof Number Important?

The Grashof number helps engineers and scientists understand and predict how heat will transfer in systems like:

  • Heating and cooling systems
  • Natural ventilation in buildings
  • Cooling of electronic devices
  • Atmospheric processes (like the rising of hot air)
Flow Regimes in Natural Convection

Low Grashof (Gr < 10⁸)

  • Laminar natural convection
  • Slow, steady upward flow of fluid
  • Heat transfer is mainly by conduction and mild convection

Moderate to High Grashof (Gr > 10⁸)

  • Turbulent natural convection
  • Chaotic fluid motion enhances mixing
  • Significantly higher heat transfer rates
Grashof Number & Heat Transfer

Combined with Prandtl Number

The product Gr·Pr helps determine the convective heat transfer regime:

Nu = C·(Gr·Pr)n

Where Nu is the Nusselt number, which gives us the convective heat transfer coefficient, and C, n are constants depending on the geometry of the system and boundary conditions.

Applications:

  • Cooling of electronic components
  • Natural ventilation systems in buildings
  • Solar panel and building surface cooling
Example Calculation

Example

Consider a vertical plate with a characteristic length (L) of 0.5 m, heated to a temperature of 80°C, while the air surrounding it is at 20°C.

Given:

  • g = 9.81 m/s²
  • β ≈ 1/300 K⁻¹
  • Ts - T∞ = 60 K
  • L = 0.5 m
  • ν = 1.6×10⁻⁵ m²/s

Gr = (9.81 × 1/300 × 60 × 0.5³) / (1.6×10⁻⁵)² ≈ 6.38 × 10⁸

This value of Gr suggests turbulent natural convection, indicating that the fluid will experience a rapid rise, leading to efficient heat transfer.
