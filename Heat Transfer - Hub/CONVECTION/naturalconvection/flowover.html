Flow Over Vertical and Horizontal Surfaces

How surface orientation affects natural convection patterns and heat transfer

Vertical Surface Flow

Characteristics:

  • Strong, continuous boundary layer development
  • Distinct rising plume formation
  • Higher heat transfer coefficients (20-50% more than horizontal)
  • Gravity directly aids fluid motion
High Efficiency

Horizontal Surface Flow

Characteristics:

  • More complex, three-dimensional flow patterns
  • Formation of cellular convection structures
  • Heat transfer depends on surface facing (up/down)
  • Generally lower heat transfer rates than vertical
Moderate Efficiency

Key Concept: Boundary Layer Development

On vertical surfaces, boundary layers grow uniformly upward, while horizontal surfaces create more complex interactions between multiple boundary layers, leading to different heat transfer characteristics.

Flow Characteristics Comparison

Governing Parameters

Surface Orientation

Angle relative to gravity vector

Nu = C(Gr·Pr)n

C and n vary with orientation:
• Vertical: C=0.59, n=0.25
• Horizontal: C=0.27, n=0.25 (heated up)
• Horizontal: C=0.54, n=0.25 (heated down)

Aspect Ratio

Height-to-width ratio effects

AR = H/W

For vertical surfaces:
• AR > 1: Dominant vertical flow
• AR < 1: Edge effects become significant
• Optimal AR ≈ 1 for many applications

Thermal Boundary Condition

Constant heat flux vs temperature

NuT ≈ 1.02 Nuq

Constant temperature (T) typically yields 2% higher Nusselt number than constant heat flux (q) for same Grashof number

Detailed Flow Patterns Analysis

Vertical Surface Dynamics

  • Boundary layer thickness increases with height: δ ∝ x1/4
  • Velocity profile peaks near surface (maximum at ~δ/3)
  • Thermal layer typically 10-30% thicker than velocity layer
  • Transition to turbulence occurs at Ra ≈ 109

Horizontal Surface Phenomena

  • Benard cells form with characteristic hexagonal patterns
  • Cell diameter typically 2-3 times fluid layer depth
  • Upward-facing: plume formation at center of cells
  • Downward-facing: stable stratification dominates

Engineering Correlations for Design

Vertical Plates

Laminar Flow (104 < Ra < 109):

Turbulent Flow (Ra > 109):

Nu = [0.825 + 0.387Ra1/6/(1 + (0.492/Pr)9/16]8/27]2

Horizontal Plates

Upper Surface Heated (or Lower Cooled):

Nu = 0.54Ra1/4 (105 < Ra < 107) Nu = 0.15Ra1/3 (107 < Ra < 1011)

Lower Surface Heated (or Upper Cooled):

Nu = 0.27Ra1/4 (105 < Ra < 1010)

Application Note

For inclined surfaces (0° < θ < 90°), multiply Grashof number by cosθ to account for reduced buoyancy component, then use vertical correlations with modified Ra.

Case Study: Solar Panel Cooling

Comparing 30° tilt (optimal for radiation) vs 90° vertical:

  • 30° tilt: Ra ≈ 5×106, Nu ≈ 20.3
  • Vertical: Ra ≈ 3×106, Nu ≈ 24.1

Despite lower Ra, vertical orientation provides 18.7% better cooling due to:

  1. Unimpeded boundary layer development
  2. Reduced hot air recirculation
  3. Better plume detachment

Example: Baseboard Heating

Vertical surface advantage in heating systems:
• Warm air rises along wall surface (boundary layer)
• Creates continuous circulation loop in room
• More efficient than floor heating (horizontal)
• Typical ΔT = 10-20°C, h ≈ 5 W/m²K

Vertical placement yields 30-40% better heat distribution than horizontal!
