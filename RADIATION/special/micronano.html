Radiation in Micro/Nano Systems

At micro and nano scales (dimensions comparable to or smaller than the characteristic thermal wavelength of radiation), the classical understanding of thermal radiation, based on the Stefan-Boltzmann law and geometric view factors, often breaks down. New phenomena emerge that significantly alter heat transfer mechanisms.

These effects include near-field radiation, where evanescent waves (non-propagating electromagnetic waves) contribute significantly to heat transfer across sub-wavelength gaps, and coherence effects, where the wave nature of light becomes important due to structural features comparable to the radiation wavelength.

Key Phenomena at Micro/Nano Scales
  • Near-Field Radiation: When two surfaces are separated by a gap smaller than the dominant thermal wavelength (typically a few micrometers at room temperature), evanescent waves tunnel across the gap, leading to heat transfer rates that can exceed the blackbody limit.
  • Evanescent Waves: These are non-propagating electromagnetic waves that decay exponentially away from a surface. In the near-field, they can couple between closely spaced objects, facilitating energy exchange.
  • Wavelength Dependence: The radiative properties of materials (emissivity, absorptivity) become highly dependent on wavelength, and the interaction with micro/nano structures can lead to selective emission/absorption.
  • Phonon-Polaritons: In polar materials, surface phonon-polaritons (coupled excitations of photons and phonons) can be excited and contribute significantly to near-field heat transfer.
  • Coherence Effects: When characteristic dimensions of structures are comparable to the thermal wavelength, interference and diffraction effects become important, influencing radiative transfer.

Applications of Micro/Nano Scale Radiation
  • Thermophotovoltaics (TPV): Designing highly efficient TPV devices by exploiting near-field radiation to enhance energy conversion from heat to electricity.
  • Thermal Diodes & Rectifiers: Creating devices that allow heat to flow preferentially in one direction, crucial for advanced thermal management.
  • Nanoscale Thermal Management: Developing new cooling solutions for microprocessors and other electronic components where traditional methods are insufficient.
  • Data Storage: Heat-assisted magnetic recording (HAMR) uses near-field optical effects to heat tiny regions for data writing.
  • Sensing & Imaging: Utilizing enhanced light-matter interactions at the nanoscale for high-resolution thermal sensing and imaging.
  • Energy Harvesting: Capturing waste heat at the nanoscale for power generation.
  • Medical Diagnostics: Developing highly sensitive thermal sensors for early disease detection.
