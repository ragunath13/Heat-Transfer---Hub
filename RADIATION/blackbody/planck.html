Planck‚Äôs Law of Spectral Distribution

Planck‚Äôs Law describes the spectral radiance of electromagnetic radiation emitted by a blackbody at a given temperature. It is a fundamental law of quantum mechanics that accurately predicts the observed spectrum of thermal radiation.

This law resolved the "ultraviolet catastrophe" predicted by classical physics and marked the birth of quantum theory.

The Planck‚Äôs Law Formula

Planck's Law gives the spectral emissive power ($E_{b\lambda}$) per unit wavelength interval as:

$E_{b\lambda}(\lambda, T) = \frac{C_1}{\lambda^5 (\exp(C_2 / (\lambda T)) - 1)}$

Where:

  • $E_{b\lambda}$: Spectral emissive power (W/m$^2 \cdot \mu m$)
  • $\lambda$: Wavelength ($\mu m$)
  • $T$: Absolute temperature of the blackbody (K)
  • $C_1 = 2 \pi h c_o^2 = 3.7418 \times 10^8 \text{ W} \cdot \mu m^4 / m^2$ (First radiation constant)
  • $C_2 = h c_o / k = 1.4388 \times 10^4 \text{ } \mu m \cdot K$ (Second radiation constant)
  • $h$: Planck's constant ($6.626 \times 10^{-34} \text{ J} \cdot s$)
  • $c_o$: Speed of light in vacuum ($2.998 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}$)
  • $k$: Boltzmann constant ($1.381 \times 10^{-23} \text{ J/K}$)

How Temperature Shapes the Spectrum

The Planck's Law curve shows that as temperature increases, the total emitted radiation increases significantly, and the peak of the emission shifts to shorter wavelengths (Wien's Displacement Law).

This explains why objects glow red at lower temperatures, then orange, yellow, and eventually white or blue as their temperature rises.

DID YOU KNOW? Planck's revolutionary idea that energy is emitted and absorbed in discrete "quanta" (packets) was initially a desperate attempt to fit experimental data, but it laid the foundation for all of modern quantum physics!
Significance and Applications
  • Birth of Quantum Theory: Planck's quantization of energy was a radical departure from classical physics and marked the beginning of quantum mechanics.
  • Ultraviolet Catastrophe Resolution: It correctly predicted the decrease in radiation intensity at very short wavelengths, solving a major problem in classical physics.
  • Temperature Measurement: Forms the basis for pyrometry, allowing non-contact temperature measurement of hot objects.
  • Astrophysics: Used to determine the temperatures of stars and other celestial bodies based on their emitted spectra.
  • Thermal Design: Essential for accurate modeling of radiative heat transfer in high-temperature engineering applications.
