Configuration (View) Factor Definition

The **configuration factor**, also known as the **view factor**, **shape factor**, or **form factor**, quantifies the fraction of radiation leaving one surface that is intercepted by another surface.

It is a purely geometric quantity, depending only on the sizes, shapes, and relative orientations of the surfaces involved, and is independent of surface properties or temperature.

Mathematical Definition

The view factor from surface $A_i$ to surface $A_j$, denoted as $F_{i \to j}$ (or $F_{ij}$), is defined by the integral:

$F_{i \to j} = \frac{1}{A_i} \int_{A_i} \int_{A_j} \frac{\cos\theta_i \cos\theta_j}{\pi r^2} dA_j dA_i$

Where:

  • $A_i$: Area of the emitting surface.
  • $A_j$: Area of the receiving surface.
  • $\theta_i$: Angle between the normal to $dA_i$ and the line connecting $dA_i$ and $dA_j$.
  • $\theta_j$: Angle between the normal to $dA_j$ and the line connecting $dA_i$ and $dA_j$.
  • $r$: Distance between $dA_i$ and $dA_j$.
  • The integral accounts for all possible pairs of differential areas on $A_i$ and $A_j$.

The view factor ranges from $0$ (no radiation from $A_i$ reaches $A_j$) to $1$ (all radiation from $A_i$ reaches $A_j$).

Key Properties of View Factors
  • Reciprocity Rule: $A_i F_{i \to j} = A_j F_{j \to i}$. This means if surface $A_i$ "sees" a fraction $F_{i \to j}$ of $A_j$, then $A_j$ "sees" a corresponding fraction $F_{j \to i}$ of $A_i$, weighted by their areas.
  • Summation Rule: For an enclosure of $N$ surfaces, the sum of view factors from any surface $A_i$ to all other surfaces (including itself if it's concave) must equal unity: $\sum_{j=1}^{N} F_{i \to j} = 1$.
  • Symmetry: If two surfaces $A_i$ and $A_j$ are symmetric with respect to each other, then $F_{i \to j} = F_{j \to i}$.

Applications of View Factors
  • Furnace Design: Calculating heat transfer between combustion chambers and workpieces.
  • Spacecraft Thermal Control: Determining radiative exchange between spacecraft components and between the spacecraft and celestial bodies.
  • Building Energy Analysis: Modeling radiative heat transfer between internal surfaces and through windows.
  • Solar Energy Systems: Designing solar collectors and concentrators to maximize the interception of solar radiation.
  • Fire Safety: Assessing radiant heat exposure from fires to nearby structures or personnel.
